The Challenge

Why IR imagery falls short

Infrared sensors are essential for night-time and adverse-weather satellite imaging — but they produce monochrome, low-contrast images that are difficult for both humans and AI models to interpret.

🌑

Monochrome & low contrast

IR sensors output single-channel thermal data. Faint edges and textures are lost — vehicles, roads, and vegetation blend into an indistinguishable grey.

🔍

Poor object recognition

Computer vision models trained on RGB data perform significantly worse on raw IR input, reducing detection accuracy for critical assets on the ground.

🌧️

Sole sensor for adverse conditions

Night, cloud cover, and smoke render optical sensors useless. IR is the only viable option — yet its output is the hardest to interpret reliably.

🎨

No semantic colour context

Without realistic RGB representations, analysts cannot distinguish water from roads, or forests from urban sprawl, with speed or confidence.

Our Approach

End-to-end processing pipeline

A five-stage deep learning pipeline — from raw GeoTIFF ingestion to high-resolution colorized output ready for downstream detection tasks.

  1. 01
    Data Acquisition
    Landsat 8/9 IR + RGB bands via USGS EarthExplorer. GDAL/Rasterio co-registration and normalisation.
    →
  2. 02
    Super-Resolution
    ESRGAN-based SR module upscales low-res IR and sharpens faint edges and texture detail.
    →
  3. 03
    IR → RGB Colorization
    Pix2Pix / CycleGAN translates enhanced IR to photorealistic RGB with pre- and post-processing.
    →
  4. 04
    Semantic Constraints
    U-Net land-cover classifier enforces correct colour mapping: water→blue, forests→green, roads→grey.
    →
  5. 05
    Evaluation
    PSNR, SSIM, FID scoring + YOLOv8 mAP improvement measurement on all output imagery.

Objectives

What we are building

Four core objectives guide every design decision in this framework.

01

Enhancement via super-resolution

Improve visibility of faint edges and textures in raw IR imagery through deep SR and adaptive sharpening — making details visible that were previously undetectable.

02

Realistic colorization

Map monochrome IR features to accurate RGB representations — thermal forest signatures become green, water bodies become blue — with zero introduced artefacts.

03

Semantic integrity

Ensure the colorization process never distorts or misrepresents actual ground truth objects. A strict zero-hallucination policy is enforced throughout the pipeline.

04

Boost downstream tasks

Output images must measurably improve YOLOv8-based object detection and segmentation accuracy compared to using raw IR input directly.

Evaluation

How we measure success

Three image-quality metrics plus task-based and qualitative checks ensure output is both visually faithful and operationally useful.

PSNR
Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio
Measures pixel-level reconstruction fidelity versus ground-truth RGB imagery.
SSIM
Structural Similarity Index
Validates that structural integrity and perceptual similarity are preserved post-translation.
FID
Fréchet Inception Distance
Evaluates photorealism of the generated RGB distribution compared to real satellite imagery.
mAP ↑
Object Detection Accuracy
YOLOv8 mean average precision improvement on colorized versus raw IR input tiles.
Tinf
Inference Time / tile
Measures scalability and real-time viability for satellite image processing at scale.
VQA
Visual Quality Assessment
Expert qualitative review — zero-hallucination policy strictly enforced on every output.
